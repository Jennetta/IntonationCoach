The most common intonation pattern in English is falling intonation, in which the pitch is lowest at the end of the sentence. Falling intonation is found in most declarative sentences (statements), as well as imperative (commands) and exclamatory sentences. For example:

Declarative: “Yesterday, I went to the store and bought groceries.”


Imperative: “Open the book and turn to page 70.”

English has several different intonation patterns for various types of questions. A basic yes-or-no question has rising intonation, meaning the pitch is the highest at the end of the sentence:

Interrogative: “Did you see the parade last weekend?”

A question that presents multiple choices and asks the listener to pick one, known as an alternative interrogative, typically has rising intonation on the first option(s) and falling intonation on the last option.

Alternative interrogative: “Should I call them, or send an email?”

Tag questions are formed when a short question is tacked onto the end of a declarative sentence. These can have either rising or falling intonation at the end, depending on the speaker’s intent; rising intonation indicates that they are genuinely asking whether the preceding statement is true, while falling intonation means that the speaker is already sure of the answer. Note the difference in intonation of the following two sentences, despite the words being the same.

Tag question: “You live in San Francisco, don’t you?”


Rhetorical tag question: “You live in San Francisco, don’t you.”
